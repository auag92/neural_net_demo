Keep in mind that the direction opposite to the gradient often does not point in the direction of the minimum (see e.g. the Rosenbrock function or banana shaped valley function ).

For stochastic gradient methods (such as the ones you mention), its even worse: following the direction of the negative gradient often looks like a random walk at small scale (image from Deeplearning4j Updaters Explained ):

For AdaGrad, the individual derivatives are divided by the L2 norm of the (recent) history of derivatives for a given parameter. The AdaGrad Wikipedia section notes that extreme parameter updates get dampened, while parameters that get few or small updates receive higher learning rates.

An example of a loss function which can lead to extreme gradients (gradient explosion) was shown in this paper by Pascanu, Mikolov and Bengio:

Youll see that when coming close to the vertical wall the method bounces the next iteration quite far away if the gradient is not dampened. In this example they considered simple one hidden unit model and thus have only two parameters to learn. But in general such situations can arise for any subset of the parameters to learn.

For Adam, the parameter updates have a term where the gradient component is divided by a term which involves the second moment of the gradient history.

This can be motivated by the fact that when you are close to the minimum and your learning rate is too high, you will overshoot the minimum all the time (jump over the minimum). Youll also notice that the spread in derivative values (hence the use of the second moment of the history of derivatives) is relatively large compared to its magnitude (in fact every time one overshoots the minimum the sign of the derivative changes). Intuitively it thus makes sense divide the the gradient updates by the spread in recent gradient values.

On the other hand, when you are far away from the minimum and recent gradients show the same trend in direction (for all or a subset of parameters), the spread of these is relatively small, they mostly agree about the direction in which the next step should be.
